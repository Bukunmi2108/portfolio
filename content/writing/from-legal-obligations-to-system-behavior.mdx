> **Working paper — January 2026.** This page summarizes the argument; the full
> text is forthcoming.

This paper examines the gap between legal requirements — transparency, bias
mitigation, risk management — and how AI systems are actually designed and
maintained in practice.

It argues that regulatory frameworks risk producing **"compliance rituals"**
rather than meaningful risk reduction when they assume legal obligations can be
cleanly translated into technical constraints. In real systems, the translation
is lossy: obligations are negotiated against latency budgets, evaluation
coverage, data availability, and the limits of what current models can reliably
do. Where the translation breaks, organizations tend to satisfy the letter of
the obligation with artifacts — documentation, attestations, dashboards —
without changing the behavior the obligation was meant to govern.

The working paper develops this argument from the vantage point of building
legal-AI systems in regulated, high-stakes domains, and asks what regulation
would need to look like to bind to system behavior rather than to paperwork.
